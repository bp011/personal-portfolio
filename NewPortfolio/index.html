Project: ColoChat

ColoChat is an interactive web-app that is designed to bring awareness and understanding to Aboriginal languages and culture in a new medium. ColoChat is created to be fun and interactive and to provide a more memorable alternative to in-class lessons for school children from grades 5-9. In the absence of the availability of an Aboriginal language teacher, Colo is readily available to provide translations and quizzes Aboriginal language.The purpose of ColoChat is to spark an interest in students and motivate them to further explore the topic.

My role in the project was project coordinator and design-lead. This involved designing the general site functionality and interactions, then translating these into static mock-ups using Adobe Illustrator and implementing these through CSS. I also designed and created our chatbot character, Colo.

Design Evolution

Over the course of the project, the design went through a series of iterations as a result of added functionality and changes based on user feedback.

Part A - Developing the Initial Concept

Product Requirements

  • Implement an engaging web-application.
  • Present one or more datasets from State Library of Queensland (SLQ) in an interactive manner.

My Contribution

  • Created the intial idea of using a language data set to form a chatbot.
  • Researched current Aboriginal language learning and revival platforms.
  • Lead team activities for brainstorming the main functionality of ColoChat.
  • Designed the user interfaces (UI) including layout, colours and imagery.
  • Designed Colo, our chatbot character.

Reflection

Part A was very successful as we made a strong start on the project. Having a detailed design direction and plans for development gave us a clear direction for Part B. Our approach to split the work required based on a task breakdown I had created was very effective. My main concern is that none of us knew all of the research to an in-depth level which may have restricted our understanding later on. Based on tutor feedback, to improve on Part A we could have concised the research and better separated the features and interactivity. These points were lated improved upon in the final design report in Part C. In terms of team work we needed to devise a better strategy for completing the report, as the work load was not completed equally due to underestimating the time required for editing, compiling and formatting.

Ideation

Producing an Aboriginal language chatbot was our intial idea. However, to ensure we were considering a range of areas in the design space, based on my experience tutoring DECO1100, I suggested to the team to brainstorm using concept cards. In the end, we chose the chatbot as it was the idea we were most enthusiastic towards, however, using our other ideas we were able to improve upon the concept. My work in this stage involved completing background research on Aboriginal language revival and designing the user experience, layout and interactivity of the web application.

Research

The most prominent challenge is the lack of human-resources e.g. teaches and translators, which has made it difficult for the current platforms to provide a personalised, flexible, and time and cost-efficient option. This influenced our ideation processes as we wanted to make a more accessible language-learning experience.


To assist with developing our concept, myself and another teammate met with Professor Greg Dickson, a specialist in Aboriginal languages, Indigenous languages, Bilingual education, Endangered languages. Originally, we had assumed we could be a language-learning platform like Duolingo but for the Aboriginal languages provided by the SLQ data. However, after talking to Dr Dickson, we arrived at the realisation that not only was this out of scope, but there is also hesitation towards trying to solve cultural problems with technology. Therefore, we decided it would be more effective to have ColoChat bring awareness to students and spark interest in Aboriginal Languages, and then direct them to more robust platforms such as local language centres.

Part B - Creating the Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

MVP Description

  • The visual layout of ColoChat had been nearly fully implemented. The CHAT page and the HELP page have content, while the INFO page had not yet been completed. At this stage, the pages were only responsive for standard desktop displays and did not have tablet or mobile-friendly versions.

My Contribution

Reflection

User-testing showed that Part B was also very successful. At this stage we had completed a substantial MVP compared to most teams which included all of our main features working. This meant we were able to use Part C to refine the MVP into a completed product. The main improvment needing ot be made to the product were adding mobile compatibility, fixing bugs in the chatbot dialogue, refining the usability and understandibility and implementing advanced features. The main process recommendations were in regard to group management and include ensuring everyone is contributing equally. The extent of this issue became very apparent in Part B and unfortunately was not fixed in later stages. The handling of this issue could have been improved by talking to the course coordinator earlier on.

Trade Show Result

During the tradeshow, users had a very positive experience trying our product (likely because of the loolies and free stickers). Many users were enticed by the visually appealing and friendly design of ColoChat and mentioned they liked Colo's cute and noticeable design. Our team also worked hard to invite users walking past and to assist users who had questions or problems to ensure each user's entire experience.


There were a number of positive yet unexpected user interactions. For example, originally, we had envisioned Colo to be used individually, yet many users tested ColoChat in pairs and had a lot of fun chatting and laughing with Colo. There was also no one observed who skipped the intro, showing how it was a valuable feature for first time users. Furthermore, some feedback we got from users was, “how is Colo so intuitive with the responses?”, “what API are you using?”. This illustrated how the decision to use the Google API (DialogFlow) enabled us to bring Colo alive and give it a personality of its own.

Reflection

Overall, ColoChat was very successful, and this was reflected by the number of people who enjoyed using our site at the trade show which led to us winning the People’s Choice Award. There are no major changes that would be made to the final product, however, there are a number of features which could be added in future iterations including speech-to-text support. In terms of the process taken in Part C, the work flow was effectively divided and problems were resolved in a very time-efficient manner. We did encounter problems with one of the APIs we were using, DialogFlow, however, this was an external problem that meant we could only wait after contacting the support team. An improvement to the process taken in Part C could have been to have team members work on additional features outside of their general responsibilities. This was completed to some extent but could be furthered if the project is continued.

Overall UI choices

ColoChat's UI is designed to be fun, inviting and memorable. From the moment you enter the site you meet Colo and undergo a short, interactive introduction to Colo's features. We've focused on creating a personal experience which is dynamic and different eveytime you use the site. The colours are bright to be eye-catching and keep the user engaged, and the content is simple and easy to understand.

Feedback on UI and Interactivity

Multiple feedback sessions were held to gain insight into the intuitiveness of the design and to identify problems and bugs. These sessions were run by asking a user (all test users were university IT students) to interact with the site with limited explanation and without prompts. This feedback is discussed in the Final Design Report.

Additon of Introductory Sequence

To guide users on how to interact with Colo, an introductory sequence with an optional walkthrough was added. This walkthrough was storyboarded by me, and then implemented by two other team members. Feedback showed this was very effective in helping users to better understand and navigate through the site.

Course Reflection

Expectations & Recommendations

‘I expect this course to be a cross of web development and design thinking, so in other words we'll be making a website that is meaningful and usable.’ - My first journal entry

To produce a meaningful website, the designers should have a deep understanding of their users and complete sufficient user-testing to ensure usability issues are identified and removed. From my experience testing other teams’ websites, many sites were confusing to use and did not have a clearly communicated purpose or context.

Below I have listed a few factors which I believe contributed to this issue.

  • The course did not encourage and inspire students to explore beyond requirements for the reports. Like my own group, I assume that in Part A most groups only completed surface-level research and did not thoroughly understand the habits and motivations of the target users but merely looked for similar sites for inspirations and made baseless assumptions. For example, as the designer I assumed that bright colours would engage children, however, I could have done further research to look at the psychology behind different colours and their effect on children. A recommendation is to incorporate the exploration of more complex research and design reasoning as a higher-level objective in Part A. It would not only result in a stronger basis for Parts B and C, but would also encourage high-achieving students to go beyond first-year level expectations.

    Furthermore, the course did not scale well to students with prior website experience who wanted to go beyond expectations. Incorporating technologies was not easily accepted by the course coordinator, for example when we used an external host and NPM. If this had not been accepted it would have limited the success of our project. Thankfully, however, we were able to use it.
  • The large assessment focus prompted teams to use a ‘divide and conquer’ approach. Although this models a realistic approach to completing a team design project, this meant each team member likely only experienced roughly a quarter of the design process. It also meant learning was based on the expected report structure and not on team-directed exploration of a personalised design space. A recommendation is to hold an activity where each team member must present orally to the team their research or to include more individual assessment.
  • My general observation was that teams were generally unwilling to initiate user-testing and didn’t actively seek feedback from peers. During contact session, very few times did I observe teams asking other students for feedback unless it was an activity directed by the tutors. Even during the paper-prototyping and demo-testing interactions were still limited, with many teams not having developed sufficient ways of gaining and recording feedback. I believe this was due to the generally low level of ambitiousness from teams. Throughout the entire design and development stages of ColoChat, my team constantly asked for feedback and looked for users to test our prototypes. This was an essential factor to our success as it helped us to eliminate bugs, particularly with the chatbot.

    • ‘That I will learn a lot about personal and team management skills.’ - My first journal entry

    My team had major issues with a team member not contributing equally in the project. Despite the rest of the team’s efforts to fix this problem, in the end it wasn’t fixed but instead compensated for. Despite our success at the tradeshow, this was the unfortunate reality and the course itself did very little to assist us. Although tutors were willing to discuss the issues and in the final weeks we had a team meeting with the course coordinator, in the end it was up to us to push for PAF scaling and we chose not to even though I believe in all fairness we should have.

    The pressure to condemn a team member’s poor performance shouldn’t be placed on teams in a first-year course. Instead, appropriate measures such as regular peer performance evaluations should have been in place to formally identify the problem. A recommendation is to use regular formative PAF rounds to identify teams earlier on which require further intervention from course staff.

Learning Experience

Although I didn't learn much from the course activities, I did learn a lot by incorporating new technology into ColoChat's implementation and design. Previously, my experience with Adobe Illustrator was limited to illustrations and basic logos. After designing Colo, the static mock-ups and the poster, my skill-level with Illustrator has greatly improved. Similarly, before this project I had not used Vue.js and SCSS. Incorporating this into the project challenged me to learn new frameworks and tools which I can now apply to future projects.

Regarding my learning experience from course activities, I was considerably unimpressed with the course for a number of reasons.

  • Many of the contact activities felt insubstantial. Starting with a ridiculous ice-breaker set a very poor tone for the course in my opinion, as it implied the course wasn't taking the students seriously. The following activities were more relevant, however, relied on student engagement which was generally low unless participation was anonymous. With little to gain in terms of content as we were all drowning in reocurring generic ideas, and no way to remember the people we had met, I don't see much value in the activities until groups were formed. For this reason, I think we should have been allocated teams as soon as possible.
  • Gaving to saty for the entire workshop timeslot felt unnecessary. Personally, I felt that workshop content was split into two categories, (1) Introductory activities that only took part of the workshop to complete, and (2) Activities with little relevance to ColoChat. In saying this, it is understandable that the participation marks were put in place to ensure all students had adequate fundamental knowledge.

Recommendations

A summary of my recommendations can be seen below:

  • Allow for more flexibility in regard to the technologies incorporated, e.g. external hosting, frameworks, etc.
  • Give examples of professional sites with good UI and interactivity to heighten self-expectations.
  • Provide resources for the UI, e.g. Google's material design and Dribble. Observations showed that many first-years were looking for good design examples but had not heard of popular resources.
  • Incorporate more individual work into Part A.
  • Add a criteria that recognises advanced or creative research and insight.
  • Use a better ice-breaker that's relevant to web development.
  • Form teams quicker.
  • Use regular formative PAF rounds to identify groups with group problems.
  • Encourage teams to actively intiate user-testing.
